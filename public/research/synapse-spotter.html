1 The Mission

You're about to help real neuroscience research. Multiple System Atrophy (MSA) is a rare, devastating neurodegenerative disease affecting roughly 5 in 100,000 people. It progressively damages the brain's ability to control movement, blood pressure, and other automatic functions.

MSA is caused by the abnormal accumulation of a protein called alpha-synuclein (α-syn) inside brain cells called oligodendrocytes. When this protein misfolds, it forms toxic clumps known as glial cytoplasmic inclusions (GCIs) that damage and kill neurons.

2 What You'll See

Each scan shows a stylized representation of neural tissue. You'll look for patterns that distinguish healthy from damaged brain regions:

Healthy tissue: Organized, symmetrical neural pathways with even distribution of activity. Dense, connected networks. Uniform signal brightness.

Damaged tissue: Broken, asymmetric pathways. Irregular bright clumps (protein aggregates). Sparse connections. Dark voids where neurons have died. Visible inclusion bodies.

Uncertain: Use this when the scan is ambiguous. Honest uncertainty is valuable — it helps identify borderline cases that need expert review.

3 How Scoring Works

Your answer is compared against other researchers' classifications. When 3+ people agree, a consensus forms and the task is resolved.

You earn Research Points (RP) — the most prestigious currency in Echo Worlds. RP cannot be bought, only earned through contributions. Bonuses for:

• Accuracy: Matching the consensus = full RP
• Streaks: Consecutive correct answers multiply your RP (up to 3×)
• Speed: Quick, confident answers earn a 25% bonus
• Difficulty: Harder scans are worth more (1-3 RP base)

4 Controls

Click the classification buttons below each scan, or use keyboard shortcuts:
[1] = Healthy   [2] = Damaged   [3] = Uncertain

Why this matters: There is currently no cure for MSA. Early diagnosis is critical but difficult — symptoms overlap with Parkinson's disease and other conditions. By classifying neural patterns, you're helping train algorithms that could one day detect MSA earlier, when intervention might still help. Every single classification matters.

🧬 Did You Know?

Alpha-synuclein is a small protein (140 amino acids) normally found at nerve terminals. In MSA, it misfolds and aggregates inside oligodendrocytes — the cells that produce the protective myelin coating around neurons.
